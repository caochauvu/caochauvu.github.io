Mutations Continued

  • Mutation: any heritable change to the original genome.
  • Point mutation: Replacement of single nucleotide by another one.
    • Two class of point mutation.
    • Transition: same class, e.g. purine->purine.
    • Tranversion: different class, e.g. purine->pyrimidine.
  • Insertions and deletions (indel)
    • Can cause frame shifts, usually resulting in a deleterious mutation.
    • We typically use the term indel because we don't know which is ancestor strand. One sequence's insertion is another sequence's deletion.
  • Microsatellites: Repeated sequence of short motifs (e.g. \((AT)_{n}\)).
    • The indels that occur here are special since they can result in more than the 4 typical alleles corresponded to the 4 nucleotides.
    • One example: the Huntington's gene. Mutations and disease results from changes in numbers of CAG repeats in coding region.
    • Microsatellites in noncoding regions can occur in regulatory regions. They are usually conserved near promoter regions.

Genetic Drifts and Selection

Neutral mutations:

  • Can take over the whole population or disappear.
  • Happens through random mutations and fluctuations.
  • You have to account for:
    • Linkage, because even if the mutation is neutral, then it might be hitchhiking.
    • Population structure.
    • Recombination.
    • Selection.

Wright-Fisher model:

  • Wright was an American geneticist.
  • Fisher was a British geneticist who also was a statistician.
  • A mathematical framework that was called modern evolutionary synthesis
  • Random sampling.
  • N is population size. \(\mu\) is rate of mutation in a population.
  • Probability of neutral mutation fixating (fixation) is \(\frac{1}{N} = \mu\)
  • Probability of new mutation in population is \(N*\mu\).
  • Therefore in an idealized neutral situation, fixation rate = mutation rate.
  • Most de novo neutral mutation don't get fixated.

Selection:

  • Fixation rate/ probability is then \(\frac{1-3^{-2s}}{1-e^{-2Ns}}\)
  • Where \(s\) is selection coefficient.
  • Advantageous mutations (\(s>>1\)) have higher probability of fixation than neutral mutation.
  • Deleterious mutations (\(s<<1\)) have lower probability of fixation than neutral mutation.
