Notes

Why study molecular evolution?

  • To build a tree of life.

Why are phylogenetics trees important?

  • Formulate hypothesis.
  • Compare organisms.
  • Geographic and time spread.
  • To understand (human) evolution.

Why is it useful to study human evolution?

  • Incorporate into other analysis, if we know which genes are related to geographical origin then we can discount them in analysis of other traits.
  • To understand evolution of human pathogens.

Biology Review

  • Genetic "information stored in DNA
    • 4 nucleotide bases
    • Purines
    • Adenine
    • Guanine
    • Pyrimidines
      • Cytosine
      • Thymine
  • DNA form double strands
    • Watson/ Crick discovery 1953
    • Whereas RNA is usually single stranded.
  • Proteins:
    • "Sequence" of amino acids (aa)
    • 20 aa subunits
    • Fold into 3D structures
    • Difficult to predict how to fold proteins.
    • Only way to see protein folds is crystallography.
    • Central Dogma:
    • DNA--Transcription-->RNA(s)--Translation-->Protein(s)
    • Transcription:
      • In prokaryotes, whole RNA molecule translated
      • In eukaryotes, there are introns (removed during RNA splicing) and exons (what remains in mature RNA).
      • Allows for multiple possible products (proteins) from one gene.
      • So central dogma should also show this multiple possible products. Signified by (s) above.
    • Translation:
      • tRNA form from 3 mRNA bases at a time.
      • These 3 bases when read together are called codons.
      • There is redundancy in the system.
      • One AA can be coded from multiple codons.
      • Allow neutral mutation.
      • 3 stop codons, one start codons.
      • Coding sequence (CDS) is also called open reading frame (ORF).
      • Some mutations cause change in ORF, which changes AA composition and therefore the protein.
