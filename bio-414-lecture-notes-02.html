Sequencing

Sanger

  • A series of starts and stops.
  • Break DNA into smaller "reads" (500-600)
  • In a tube mix:
    • Template sequence: The thing you want to sequence.
    • Primer: A short known sequence complementary to portion of template.
    • DNA polymerase (enzyme): Will extend primer by adding nucleotides to it.
    • Free nucleotide bases (dNTPs/ddNTPs):
    • dNTPs are what the DNA polymerase add to the primer to extend it.
    • The ddNTPs will terminate the extending by a stopping the polymerase reaction.
    • The ddNTPs have a fluorescent tags that machines can read in order to sequence the genome.

Next-Gen

  • Break the long DNA into even shorter reads
    • Use to be less than 50 bases but is now larger and still increasing, not as much as 500 though.
  • Sequence separately and then match up overlapping regions, and assemble the sequence in order (genome assembly)
    • Usually about 30 bases overlapping is good, 10 is sufficient though.
  • Pros:
    • Ability to sequence a lot of small chunks in parallel.
    • You don't need a primer.
  • Cons:
    • Chunks are small, higher chance of inaccuracy.
    • More difficult to align.
    • Being done in parallel in one test tube, the reaction is less controlled and has higher rate of error (base wrongly attached).

Mutations

Terminology

  • Locus: Location of a genetic element in genome.
    • Teacher's definition: "Locus is a location in the genome-usually small DNA segment, but could be as small as one nucleotide or as large as a coding region/ gene"
  • Makers (loci): A loci where there is variation in the population.
  • Alleles: Different version of same gene (classical definition), could also refer to different variation of locus (e.g. introns).
  • Polymorphic: A locus where where there is multiple alleles.
